# 1. Data vs. Information

Understanding the distinction between these two concepts is fundamental to information systems.

- **Data (Datum):** Raw facts such as numbers, statements, or pictures. Examples include a list of items sold and their prices.
- **Process:** The transformation stage where operations like tabulation, addition, subtraction, or division are applied to data to create a greater understanding of a situation.
- **Information:** The result or product of processing data. It is meaningful, easily understood, and useful for decision-making. For example, processing raw sales data allows a manager to find total unit sales or total revenue for a specific store.
- **The Decision Cycle:** The ultimate goal of this progression is: **Data → Process → Information → Decision**.

# 2. Characteristics of Quality Information

For information to be valuable to an organization, it should possess these **11 characteristics**:

1. **Accessible:** Easy to obtain when needed.
2. **Accurate:** Error-free.
3. **Complete:** Contains all required details.
4. **Economical:** The value produced is worth the cost of gathering it.
5. **Flexible:** Can be used for multiple purposes.
6. **Relevant:** Important to the decision-maker.
7. **Reliable:** Trusted by users.
8. **Secure:** Protected from unauthorized access.
9. **Simple:** Contains only necessary details to avoid information overload.
10. **Timely:** Received when it is still useful.
11. **Verifiable:** Accuracy can be checked.

# 12. What is a System?

- **Definition:** A set of interrelated components with a clearly defined boundary, working together toward a common set of objectives.
- **Core Functions:** Input (capturing raw data), Process (conversion), and Output (transferring processed info to users).
- **Hierarchy:** Systems are often composed of **sub-systems** (specialized functions within a system) and **sub-subsystems**.
- **Open vs. Closed Systems:**
    - **Open Systems:** Exchange information, energy, or material with their environments (e.g., biological and social systems). In a computer context, they allow unrestricted access and are free to modify.
    - **Closed Systems:** Have limited access, complete control over data manipulation, and are protected from unauthorized access.

# 4. Computer-Based Information Systems (CBIS)

A **CBIS** represents an organizational and management solution based on information technology to meet environmental challenges.

## The Six Components of a CBIS

1. **Hardware:** Physical devices and materials used for processing (e.g., CPU, memory, and peripherals like scanners, printers, and monitors).
2. **Software:** Computer programs that govern operations. This includes **System Software** (controlling CPU functions) and **Application Software** (for specific tasks).
3. **Databases:** Organized collections of data. They are managed using **Database Management Systems (DBMS)** like MySQL or Oracle.
4. **Telecommunications and Networks:** The electronic transmission of signals (voice, data) that links systems into LANs (Local Area Networks) or WANs (Wide Area Networks).
5. **Procedures:** Policies and methods for using the system. This includes access policies and task-specific methods.
6. **People:** Includes **End Users** (who use the info) and **IS Specialists** (System Analysts, Software Developers, and System Operators).

# 5. Why Study Information Systems?

- **End-User Perspective:** Enhances personal and group productivity and increases opportunities for success by identifying management problems.
- **Enterprise Perspective:** Vital for business success, ensuring efficient operations, effective management, and **competitive advantage**.

# 6. IS Careers and Challenges

The field offers diverse career paths, including:

- **Technical Roles:** Software Developer, Database Administrator, Network Administrator, Web Developer.
- **Strategic/Managerial Roles:** Systems Analyst, Business Analyst, Chief Information Officer (CIO), Chief Technology Officer (CTO), Project Manager.
