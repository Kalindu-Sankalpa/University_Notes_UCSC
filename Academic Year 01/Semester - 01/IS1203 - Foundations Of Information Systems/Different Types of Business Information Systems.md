In an organization, information systems are typically categorized from two perspectives: the **functional perspective** (what the system does) and the **constituency perspective** (who the system serves).

# 1. The Functional Perspective

This view identifies systems based on the major business function they support.

- **Sales and Marketing Systems:** Help identify customers, develop and promote products/services, and process orders. Examples include **Order Processing** (Operational), **Price Analysis** (Management), and **Sales Trend Forecasting** (Strategic).
- **Manufacturing and Production Systems:** Manage the firm's long-term manufacturing goals and day-to-day production tasks. Examples include **Machine Control** (Operational), **Production Planning** (Management), and **Facility Location** (Strategic).
- **Finance and Accounting Systems:** Track the flow of funds and oversee financial resources. Examples include **Accounts Receivable** (Operational), **Budgeting** (Management), and **Profit Planning** (Strategic).
- **Human Resource Systems (HRIS):** Monitor recruitment, training, and employee compensation. Examples include **Training and Development** (Operational), **Compensation Analysis** (Management), and **HR Planning** (Strategic).

# 2. The Constituency Perspective

This view identifies systems based on the organizational level and management group they serve.

- **Transaction Processing Systems (TPS):** The most basic systems that serve the **operational level** by automating repetitive, routine tasks like payroll or order entry. They are critical data sources for other systems.
- **Office Automation Systems (OAS):** Designed to meet the knowledge needs of data workers for handling documents, scheduling, and communication (e.g., Microsoft Office, Google Workspace).
- **Management Information Systems (MIS):** Provide **middle managers** with reports on current performance to support routine, structured decision-making. They focus entirely on internal events and historical data.
- **Decision Support Systems (DSS):** Used by managers and analysts for unique, rapidly changing problems that are not specified in advance. They have high analytical power and use both internal and external data.
- **Executive Support Systems (ESS):** Serve the **strategic level (senior management)** to address non-routine decisions requiring judgment and evaluation. Information is often delivered via high-level **dashboards** or portals.

# 3. Enterprise Applications

These systems span multiple functional areas and organizational levels, focusing on executing business processes across the entire firm.

- **Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP):** A suite of integrated software modules with a **common central database** that allows data to be shared across all departments (Finance, HR, Sales, etc.).
- **Supply Chain Management (SCM) Systems:** Coordinate activities like purchasing, logistics, and resource management to move products from suppliers to the end consumer efficiently.
- **Customer Relationship Management (CRM) Systems:** Cross-functional systems that integrate all customer-facing processes to provide a complete view of the customer at every "touchpoint".
- **Knowledge Management Systems (KMS):** Collect and link internal and external knowledge to support processes for creating, storing, and distributing expertise throughout the organization.
