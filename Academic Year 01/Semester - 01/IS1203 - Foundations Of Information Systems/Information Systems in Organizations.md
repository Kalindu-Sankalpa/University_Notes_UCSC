# 1. Defining the Organization

- **Basic Definition:** An organization is a formal collection of resources and people structured and managed to accomplish a set of goals.
- **The Technical View:** From a technical perspective, it is a formal, legal, and social structure that processes resources (inputs) to produce products or services (outputs).
- **Open System:** Organizations are considered **open systems**, meaning they both affect and are affected by their surrounding environment.

# 2. The Relationship Between Organizations and IT

There is a complex, two-way relationship between organizations and information technology. This relationship is influenced by several **mediating factors**:

- **Environment:** External pressures from competitors, customers, and regulations.
- **Culture:** The "personality" of the organization, consisting of shared values and beliefs.
- **Structure:** The hierarchy and arrangement of sub-units.
- **Business Processes:** The series of activities performed to achieve goals.
- **Politics and Management Decisions:** Internal dynamics and leadership choices.

# 3. Business Processes and the Value Chain

- **Business Process:** A series of logical activities and steps (often repetitive) that use resources to produce a specific outcome or result. These are categorized into **Core**, **Management**, and **Support** processes.
- **The Value Chain:** A series (chain) of activities involved in creating a product or performing a service.
    - **Primary Activities:** Directly related to production and distribution (e.g., Inbound Logistics, Operations, Marketing/Sales).
    - **Support Activities:** Make the delivery of primary activities possible (e.g., HR Management, Technology Development, Procurement).
- **Profitability Goal:** The goal is to create more value than the cost of the activities involved. **Gross Profit = Value Created - Cost of Creating Value**.

# 4. Organizational Structure and Culture

- **Organizational Structure:** This defines how sub-units relate to the overall organization. Major types include:
    - **Traditional:** A hierarchical "managerial pyramid" where department heads report to top-level managers.
    - **Project/Team:** Centered on major products, services, or specific work groups.
    - **Matrix:** A structure where employees may report to multiple managers (e.g., both a functional manager and a project manager).
    - **Virtual:** Employs geographically dispersed business units where people may never meet face-to-face.
- **Organizational Culture:** Shared values and "unwritten rules" that govern how employees interact and approach work. Culture can influence the successful usage of IS, and IS can, in turn, influence organizational culture.

# 5. Organizational Change: BPR vs. Continuous Improvement

Information systems are often used to address the need for organizational change, driven by internal (growth) or external (market dynamics) factors.

- **Business Process Re-engineering (BPR):** A **radical redesign** of business processes and structures to achieve a breakthrough in results. It is usually top-down and broad in scope.
- **Continuous Improvement:** A routine, gradual approach where workers constantly seek minor ways to improve processes, such as increasing customer loyalty or reducing dissatisfaction.

# 6. Gaining Competitive Advantage

A competitive advantage is a significant, long-term benefit a firm has over its competitors, allowing for greater sales or margins.

- **Porter’s Five Forces Model:** Firms use IS to counter five competitive forces:
    1. Bargaining power of customers.
    2. Threat of new entrants.
    3. Bargaining power of suppliers.
    4. Threat of substitute products.
    5. Competitive rivalry within the industry.
- **Strategic Planning:** IS helps organizations achieve strategies like **Cost Leadership** (reducing costs), **Differentiation** (making products stand out), or **Niche Strategies** (targeting narrow customer segments).
