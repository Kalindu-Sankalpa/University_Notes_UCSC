# 1 What is Agile?

Agile is a software development approach focused on **flexibility, collaboration, customer feedback, and rapid delivery**. Unlike traditional methods that deliver the entire product at the end, Agile builds software **incrementally and iteratively** from the start. It is highly popular in dynamic environments where requirements are fast-changing.

# 2 The Agile Manifesto

Created in **2001** by 17 developers in Utah, the Agile Manifesto serves as the foundational philosophy for all Agile methods.

## A. The 4 Core Values:

The Manifesto prioritizes the items on the left over the items on the right:

1. **Individuals and interactions** over processes and tools.
2. **Working software** over comprehensive documentation.
3. **Customer collaboration** over contract negotiation.
4. **Responding to change** over following a plan.

# B. The 12 Principles:

These principles guide teams on how to work effectively. Key highlights include:

- **Customer Satisfaction:** Highest priority through early and continuous delivery of valuable software.
- **Welcoming Change:** Even late in development, change is harnessed for the customer's advantage.
- **Frequent Delivery:** Working software is delivered in weeks rather than months.
- **Daily Cooperation:** Business people and developers must work together daily.
- **Motivated Individuals:** Build projects around trusted, supported people.
- **Face-to-Face Conversation:** The most efficient way to convey information.
- **Primary Measure of Progress:** Actual working software.
- **Sustainable Development:** Sponsors, developers, and users should maintain a constant pace indefinitely.
- **Technical Excellence:** Continuous attention to good design enhances agility.
- **Simplicity:** The art of maximizing the amount of work _not_ done.
- **Self-Organizing Teams:** The best architectures and designs emerge from teams that manage themselves.
- **Regular Reflection:** The team periodically reflects on how to become more effective and adjusts accordingly.

# 3 Agile Development Life Cycle (ADLC)

Agile operates in short, time-boxed cycles called **Sprints or Iterations**, typically lasting 1–4 weeks. Each sprint functions as a mini-project that delivers an improved version of the product.

## The Phases of ADLC:

1. **Concept:** The product owner determines the project's scope and feasibility.
2. **Inception:** The team is formed, resources are provided, and initial UI mockups and architecture are built.
3. **Iteration (Development):** The longest phase where developers write code to build bare functionality.
4. **Release:** Quality Assurance (QA) performs final testing, user training occurs, and the product is deployed.
5. **Maintenance:** Ongoing support and updates for the live product.
6. **Retirement:** The software is phased out or replaced.

## Central Activities in a Sprint:

- **Requirement Gathering:** Understanding user needs through user stories and workshops.
- **Design:** Turning abstract needs into tangible wireframes and mockups.
- **Coding & Development:** Building software incrementally in short cycles.
- **Testing/QA:** Performing unit, integration, and User Acceptance Testing (UAT) to find faults early.
- **Deployment:** Delivering the product increment to the client after each sprint.
- **Feedback:** Learning from the user experience to improve the system.

# 4 Pros and Cons of Agile

## Advantages:

- **Happy Customers:** Due to continuous delivery and involvement.
- **High Adaptability:** Welcomes changes at any stage.
- **Faster Delivery:** Small, frequent releases provide value sooner.
- **Reduced Risk:** Issues are identified and fixed early.

## Disadvantages:

- **Lack of Documentation:** Heavy focus on code can lead to sparse written records.
- **Team Expertise:** Success depends on highly experienced, self-managed teams.
- **Scope Creep:** Excessive changes can distract from the original project goals.



# 5 Agile vs. Traditional (Plan-Driven) Approaches

| Feature               | Agile Approach              | Traditional (e.g., Waterfall)     |
| --------------------- | --------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| **Development Style** | Iterative & Incremental     | Linear & Sequential               |
| **Flexibility**       | Very High (welcomes change) | Low (hard to change once started) |
| **Requirements**      | Evolve over time            | Must be fixed and clear upfront   |
| **User Involvement**  | Continuous collaboration    | Limited to initial requirements   |
| **Delivery**          | Frequent, small releases    | One big release at the end        |

# 6 When to Use Agile?

Agile is recommended for projects that have **changing or unclear requirements**, require **fast delivery**, or are **user-focused** (like UI/UX heavy apps). It is also ideal for custom system development within an organization.
