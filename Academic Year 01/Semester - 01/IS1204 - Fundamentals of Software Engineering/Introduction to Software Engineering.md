# 1 Defining Software

In software engineering, software is much more than just the executable code. It is defined as a combination of three essential components:

- **Computer Programs:** The instructions provided to the computer to perform tasks.
- **System Documentation:** Records produced during the development process, such as design specifications and requirements.
- **User Documentation:** Manuals and guides provided to the customer to explain how to use the product.

## Key Characteristics:

- Software is **engineered or developed**, not manufactured in a traditional factory sense.
- **Failure Rates:** Unlike hardware, which "wears out," software failure rates should theoretically remain constant. However, in reality, failure rates often **increase over time** because changes and modifications made to the system introduce new side effects and defects.
- **A Notorious Truth:** As noted by Paul Ehrlich, “To err is human, but to really foul things up you need a computer”.

# 2 Types of Software

Software is generally categorized by its function and its intended market.

## A. Functional Categories

- **System Software:** Essential low-level software (like Operating Systems and Device Drivers) that manages hardware and provides a platform for applications.
- **Application Software:** Often referred to as **"non-essential software"** because its absence does not affect the system's core functioning. These are end-user programs like web browsers, spreadsheet software, and database software.
    - _Example:_ **Word Processing Software** (e.g., Microsoft Word) allows for the editing, saving, and creation of documents.

## B. Market Categories

- **Generic (Packaged):** Stand-alone systems produced by a development organization and sold on the open market (e.g., Adobe Photoshop).
- **Customized (Bespoke):** Software specifically commissioned by a particular customer to meet their unique needs (e.g., Air Traffic Control systems).

# 3 What is Software Engineering?

Software Engineering is a **systematic, disciplined approach** to the development and maintenance of quality software products. It differs from other engineering fields because:

- **Intangibility:** Software is a "brain product" and cannot be physically touched, making it hard to visualize and specify.
- **Volatility:** The environment and requirements for software change rapidly.
- **Testing Complexity:** It is impossible to test every possible state or path within a piece of software.

# 4 The Economics of Software

The cost of software often far outweighs the cost of the hardware it runs on.

- **Maintenance vs. Development:** For custom systems, the costs of **software maintenance usually exceed development costs**.
- **Cost Distribution:** In a typical software lifecycle, **maintenance accounts for approximately 67% of total costs**, while the actual coding phase only accounts for about 7%.

# 5 Project Outcomes and Failures

Research by the Standish Group shows that software projects often struggle to meet their goals:

- **Successful (29%):** Completed on time and on budget with all features.
- **Challenged (52%):** Operational, but over budget, late, or with limited features.
- **Failed (19%):** Cancelled before completion.

**Why do projects fail?** The single largest contributor to project failure is **Poor Requirements Definition (50%)**, followed by inadequate risk management (17%) and poor communication (14%). When these elements are ignored, it can lead to **software disasters**, resulting in massive financial losses or even human fatalities.


# 6 Ethics and Professional Responsibility

Software engineers have a professional responsibility that goes beyond technical competence. This involves:

- Recognizing **ethical issues** critical to the field.
- Adhering to professional standards for **technical excellence** and **sustainable development**.
- Ensuring the safety and dependability of systems that impact human lives.
