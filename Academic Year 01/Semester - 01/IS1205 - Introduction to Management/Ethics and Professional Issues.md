In the transition toward a **Knowledge Society**, where knowledge—rather than capital or labor—is the basic economic resource, the importance of ethics in management becomes paramount.

# 1. Defining Ethics

**Ethics** is the practice of making a **principled choice between right and wrong**. Managers often face two types of ethical choices:

- **Choosing Right from Wrong:** For example, deciding between profit-making and the privacy of information, or between lying about a project and telling the truth.
- **Choosing Right from Right:** These are dilemmas where two positive values conflict, such as **security vs. convenience**, or fully testing software vs. meeting an on-time delivery deadline.

# 2. Legality vs. Ethicality

Not everything that is legal is ethical, and vice versa. Managers use a matrix to categorize actions:

- **Quadrant I (Ethical & Legal):** e.g., Driving with a license, proper salary structures.
- **Quadrant II (Ethical but Not Legal):** e.g., Casual adoption of a homeless child.
- **Quadrant III (Not Ethical but Legal):** e.g., Breaking a verbal promise, nepotism, or certain cases of the death penalty.
- **Quadrant IV (Not Ethical & Not Legal):** e.g., Theft, murder, sexual harassment, or physical abuse.

# 3. Informal Guidelines for Ethical Decision-Making

When formal policies aren't enough, managers can use these "tests" to evaluate their actions:

- **The Mom Test:** Would you be proud or ashamed to tell your mother what you did?
- **The TV Test:** How would you feel if your action was described on TV to millions of viewers?
- **The Smell Test:** Does your instinct or "gut feeling" tell you something is wrong?
- **The Other Person’s Shoes Test:** If the roles were reversed, would you be happy if this act was done to you?
- **The Market Test:** Would the majority of society praise or criticize this action?

# 4. Computer and Internet Ethics

The sources highlight specific responsibilities in the digital age:

- **10 Commandments of Computer Ethics:** These include rules such as "Thou shalt not use a computer to harm other people" and "Thou shalt not interfere with other people's computer work".
- **Conducting Business on the Internet:** Before taking action, managers should ask if they are coloring facts to bias a message, if anyone will be harmed, or if they feel the need to rationalise their behavior. If the answer to any of these is "yes," the action should be reconsidered.
