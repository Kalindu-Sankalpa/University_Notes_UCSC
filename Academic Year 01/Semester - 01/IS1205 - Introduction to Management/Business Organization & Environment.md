# 1. Social Transformation (Evolution of Society)

Society has evolved through several distinct stages, each characterized by different primary economic resources and organizational structures:

- **Agricultural Society (Pre-1750):** Built on the "six pillars" of soil, water, climate, seeds, tools, and peasants.
- **Industrial Society (1850–1970):** Defined by mass production, standardization, and synchronization (the "9 to 5" workday). It prioritized maximization, centralization, and optimization.
- **Information Society (1970–2000):** Shifting focus toward data and digital communication.
- **Knowledge Society (2000–Present):** An era where **knowledge** is the basic economic resource, rather than capital or labor. Knowledge workers play the central role, and industries become knowledge-intensive (e.g., using drones and satellite photos in agriculture).
- **AI Society:** The emerging stage characterized by **smart products** that can make independent decisions based on situational data.

# 2. Historical Contributions to Organizational Structure

The management structures of the industrial age were shaped by three key figures:

- **Adam Smith (1776):** Introduced the **Division of Labor**, emphasizing specialization to increase productivity.
- **Henry Ford (1890):** Developed **Assembly Lines**, which brought the work to the worker, further standardizing production.
- **Alfred Sloan:** Applied the division of labor to **management structures**, focusing on large-scale strategic planning and monitoring divisional performance.

## Analysis of Division of Labor:

- **Advantages:** Increases skill through repetition, reduces training time, minimizes idle time between tasks, and improves overall product quality.
- **Disadvantages:** Workers may feel a lack of responsibility for the end result, leading to lower motivation and higher absenteeism. It can also cause physical issues like repetitive motion disorders.

# 3. The Business Environment

Organizations must navigate two interrelated environments:

- **Internal Environment:** Factors within the organization, such as **employees**, board of directors, and internal structures (HR, Finance, IT, Production).
- **External Environment:** Outside factors that impact the business, including **customers, government, suppliers, competitors**, financial institutions, and interest groups.

# 4. Strategic Analysis Tools

Managers use several frameworks to understand their environment and gain a competitive advantage:

- **PEST Analysis:** A tool to analyze the macro-environmental factors: **P**olitical, **E**conomical, **S**ocial, and **T**echnological.
- **SWOT Analysis:** A framework for identifying an organization’s **S**trengths and **W**eaknesses (internal) alongside **O**pportunities and **T**hreats (external).
- **Porter’s Five Forces:** A model used to analyze the industry's attractiveness and competitive intensity:
    1. **Threat of New Entrants:** How easy it is for new competitors to enter the market.
    2. **Bargaining Power of Suppliers:** The pressure suppliers can exert on the business.
    3. **Bargaining Power of Buyers (Customers):** The influence customers have on pricing and quality.
    4. **Threat of Substitutes:** The likelihood of customers switching to alternative products.
    5. **Rivalry Among Competitors:** The intensity of competition between existing firms in the industry.
