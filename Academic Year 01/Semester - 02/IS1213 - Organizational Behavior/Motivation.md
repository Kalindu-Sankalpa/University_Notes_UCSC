>**Motivation**, is a cornerstone of Organizational Behavior, representing the "driving force" behind employee performance and productivity. Understanding how to motivate employees is essential for managers who wish to achieve organizational goals.

---
# Defining Motivation

Motivation is the process that accounts for an individual’s **intensity, direction, and persistence** of effort toward attaining a goal, specifically an organizational one. It is viewed as the result of the interaction between the **individual and the situation**.

The three key elements are:

- **Intensity:** How hard a person tries.
- **Direction:** Effort that is channeled toward, and consistent with, organizational goals.
- **Persistence:** How long a person can maintain their effort.

# Early Theories of Motivation

While these theories were developed in the 1950s and may not be entirely valid today, they form the basis for contemporary theories and are still used by managers.

- **Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs:**
  Proposes a hierarchy of five needs—**Physiological, Safety, Social (Belongingness/Love), Esteem, and Self-Actualization**. As each lower-level need is satisfied, the next one becomes dominant.

- **McGregor’s Theory X and Theory Y:**
  Contrast two views of human beings. **Theory X** is negative, assuming workers dislike work and avoid responsibility; **Theory Y** is positive, assuming workers are self-directed and enjoy work.

- **Herzberg’s Two-Factor Theory:**
  Suggests that satisfaction and dissatisfaction are separate constructs. **Hygiene factors** (like salary and working conditions) prevent dissatisfaction, while **motivators** (like growth and achievement) actually cause satisfaction.

- **McClelland’s Three Needs Theory:**
  Focuses on three main needs: the **Need for Achievement (nAch)**, the **Need for Power (nPow)**, and the **Need for Affiliation (nAff)**. High achievers prefer jobs with personal responsibility, feedback, and moderate risk.

# Contemporary Theories of Motivation

These theories utilize older concepts to provide a deeper understanding of motivation.

- **Self-Determination Theory:**
  Proposes that people prefer to feel they have control over their actions. It highlights that **intrinsic rewards** (internal satisfaction) can be undermined by **extrinsic rewards** (like money) if a task begins to feel like an obligation.

- **Goal-Setting Theory:**
  Argues that **specific and difficult goals**, accompanied by feedback, lead to higher performance.

- **Equity Theory:**
  Based on the idea that employees compare their **outcome-to-input ratios** with those of relevant others. Perception of inequity can lead to tension and motivate individuals to act to correct the situation (e.g., slacking off or quitting).

- **Expectancy Theory (Vroom):**
  The most widely accepted theory; it argues that the strength of an individual's tendency to act depends on their **expectation** that the act will lead to a desired outcome. It focuses on three relationships: Effort-Performance, Performance-Reward, and Rewards-Personal Goals.

- **Organizational Justice:**
  A refinement of equity theory that considers the overall perception of fairness in the workplace, including **distributive justice** (fairness of outcomes), **procedural justice** (fairness of the process), and **interactional justice** (being treated with dignity).

# Global and Managerial Implications

The sources note that motivation theories are often **culture-bound**. For example, the order of Maslow's needs is not universal, and the desire for equity varies globally. However, the desire for **interesting work** appears to be a universal motivator.

For managers, understanding these theories helps in **selecting, promoting, and fine-tuning jobs** to fit employee abilities and drive better performance
