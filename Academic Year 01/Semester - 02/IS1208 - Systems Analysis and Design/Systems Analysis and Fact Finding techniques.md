# What is System Analysis?

**Systems Analysis** is the formal study of a system and its various components. It is a necessary prerequisite to systems design because it focuses on **what the system must do** (the business problem) rather than how it will be technically implemented. This process is primarily driven by the business concerns of system owners and users, addressing their needs regarding knowledge, processes, and communication.

The **Systems Analyst** serves as the facilitator for this phase. They are responsible for:

- Identifying and understanding the problem.
- Analyzing information needs and business requirements.
- Identifying potential solution requirements and alternatives.
- **Bridging the communication gap** between nontechnical business stakeholders and technical people like programmers.

## Requirements Discovery and Analysis

A major part of systems analysis is **Requirements Discovery**, which is the process used by analysts to identify, analyze, and understand what the users need. Requirements are generally categorized into two types:

- **Functional Requirements:**
	The specific services or functions the system must provide (e.g., "Lending books" in a library system).
- **Non-functional Requirements:**
	The constraints under which the system must operate, such as performance, security, and reliability.

For these requirements to be effective, they must meet specific criteria: they should be **consistent, complete, feasible, accurate, traceable, and verifiable**.

# Fact-Finding Techniques

To accurately discover these requirements, analysts use **Fact-Finding** (also known as information gathering), which is a formal process of collecting data about systems requirements. The sources identify seven primary techniques:

1. **Sampling of Existing Documentation:**
	Analysts seek out organizational charts, existing forms, files, and reports. This helps them understand the current system and identifies the formal business structure.
2. **Research and Site Visits:**
	This involves researching the specific problem through computer trade journals, reference books, and the internet to find reliable and relevant information.
3. **Observation of the Work Environment:**
	The analyst watches or participates in activities to learn about the system. While this is highly reliable and inexpensive, it can make employees feel uncomfortable or lead to them acting differently while being watched.
4. **Questionnaires:**
	These allow analysts to collect information and opinions from a large audience relatively inexpensively. They can be **free-format** (open-ended) or **fixed-format** (multiple-choice, rating, or ranking). While they allow for anonymity, they often have low response rates and do not allow for immediate clarification of vague answers.
5. **Interviews:**
	This is the **most commonly used technique**. It involves face-to-face meetings to find, verify, and clarify facts. Interviews can be **unstructured** (general goals) or **structured** (specific set of questions). While very effective for gathering feedback and motivating users, they are time-consuming and costly.
6. **Prototyping:**
	This involves building working but incomplete samples of a system to help users "see" and discover their requirements. It helps minimize the time spent on traditional fact-finding and defines more stable requirements.
7. **Joint Requirements Planning (JRP):**
	This is a highly structured group meeting or workshop where system owners, users, and IT staff meet to analyze problems and define requirements. It requires a skilled facilitator to manage mediation and negotiation.

**Ethics in Fact-Finding:** It is important to note that analysts often handle sensitive data during this process. They have a professional obligation to protect the **security and privacy** of the facts they uncover.
