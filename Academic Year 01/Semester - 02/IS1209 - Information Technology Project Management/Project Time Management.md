>[!info] Introduction
>Project Time Management (also referred to as **Project Schedule Management**) focuses on the processes required to ensure the timely completion of a project. Managers often cite delivering projects on time as one of their biggest challenges because **time is the least flexible constraint**—it passes regardless of what happens on the project.

---
# Teaching the Core Concepts

## 1. The Six Main Processes

There are six key processes involved in managing time effectively:

- **Define Activities:**
  Identifying the specific tasks that must be performed to produce deliverables. This involves expanding the WBS into a more detailed activity list.
- **Sequence Activities:**
  Identifying and documenting relationships between activities. You must determine **dependencies** (mandatory, discretionary, or external) to use critical path analysis.
- **Estimate Activity Resources:**
  Determining what resources (people, equipment, materials) and how much of each are needed.
- **Estimate Activity Duration:**
  Estimating the number of work periods needed to complete tasks. Note that **Duration** (actual time) is different from **Effort** (man-hours).
- **Develop Schedule:**
  Analyzing sequences, resource estimates, and duration to create the project schedule.
- **Control Schedule:**
  Managing changes to the project schedule.

## 2. Key Tools and Techniques

- **Network Diagrams:**
  The preferred technique for showing sequencing. There are two main formats:
	- **Arrow Diagramming Method (ADM):**
	  Activities are arrows.
	- **Precedence Diagramming Method (PDM):**
	  Activities are boxes; more popular and better at showing various dependency types (e.g., Finish-to-Start, Start-to-Start).
- **Gantt Charts:**
  Provide a standard format for displaying schedule information by listing activities and their corresponding start and finish dates in a calendar format.
- **Critical Path Method (CPM):**
  A technique used to predict total project duration. The **Critical Path** is the longest path through the network diagram and represents the earliest time the project can be completed.
- **PERT (Program Evaluation and Review Technique):**
  Used when there is high uncertainty in duration estimates. It uses a weighted average formula: $$\frac{Optimistic+4(MostLikely)+Pessimistic​}{6}$$

## 3. Schedule Compression and Control

If a project is behind, managers can use **Crashing** (adding resources for the least incremental cost) or **Fast Tracking** (performing activities in parallel that were originally planned in sequence) to shorten the schedule. Effective control requires **reality checks**—reviewing the draft schedule with the team and alerting management early if problems arise.

# Short Note for Study

## Topic: Project Time Management

- **Triple Constraint Insight:**
  Schedule issues are the main reason for project conflicts, especially in the second half of a project.
- **Milestones:**
  Significant events with zero duration used to set goals and monitor progress; they should follow **SMART** criteria (Specific, Measurable, Assignable, Realistic, Time-framed).
- **Critical Path:**
  The series of activities that determines the earliest completion date. It has **zero slack or float**.
- **Slack (Float):**
  The amount of time an activity can be delayed without delaying a succeeding activity (**Free Slack**) or the project finish date (**Total Slack**).
- **Critical Chain Scheduling:** 
  A method that accounts for limited resources and uses **buffers** (project and feeding buffers) to protect the completion date, aiming to minimize multitasking.
- **Process Tip:**
  Top managers should act as **champions** to help project managers secure the resources needed to stay on schedule.
---

**Analogy:** Project Time Management is like **choreographing a complex dance routine**. You must define every step (**Defining Activities**), decide which dancer follows another (**Sequencing**), and know how long each move takes (**Duration Estimating**). The **Critical Path** is the sequence of dancers who have no room for error; if one slips, the entire performance finishes late. **Crashing** is like bringing in extra dancers to finish a scene faster, while **Fast Tracking** is having two groups perform their parts at the same time instead of one after the other.
