# The Scoop Management Process

There are six main processes involved in managing scope:

- **Plan Scope Management:**
	Determining how the scope and requirements will be defined, validated, and controlled.
- **Collect Requirements:**
	Defining and documenting the features and functions the products must have. Managers use interviews, focus groups, surveys, and prototyping to gather this data.
- **Define Scope:**
	Developing a detailed description of the project and product. This results in a Project Scope Statement, which includes deliverables, acceptance criteria, and project exclusions.
- **Create WBS (Work Breakdown Structure):**
	Subdividing major project deliverables into smaller, more manageable components.
- **Validate Scope:**
	Formalizing acceptance of the completed project deliverables, often through customer inspection and sign-off.
- **Control Scope:**
	Monitoring the status of the project and product scope and managing changes to the scope baseline.

# The Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)

The **WBS** is considered the foundation of project management because all planning and control are based on it.

- **Decomposition:**
	This is the technique of breaking down deliverables into smaller pieces called **Work Packages** (the lowest level of the WBS).
- **The 8-80 Hour Rule:**
	A common guideline suggesting that work packages should generally not be smaller than 8 hours or larger than 80 hours.
- **WBS Dictionary:**
	A document that provides detailed information about each WBS item to ensure the team understands the required work.

# Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM)

The **RTM** is a table used to link product requirements from their origin to the deliverables that satisfy them. It ensures that all requirements are addressed and helps track their status throughout the project.

# Avoiding "Scope Creep"

The sources warn against **gold plating** (adding extra work not in the charter) and **scope creep** (the tendency for project scope to keep growing). To avoid this, projects should have a clear **Change Control System** and involve users early and often in the process.

# Short Note for Study

## Topic: Project Scope Management

- **Definition:**
	The processes required to ensure the project includes all the work required, and **only the work required**, to complete the project successfully.
- **Deliverable:**
	A product produced as part of a project, such as hardware, software, or planning documents.
- **WBS (Work Breakdown Structure):**
	A deliverable-oriented grouping of work that defines the total scope. If a task isn't in the WBS, it isn't part of the project.
- **Key WBS Approaches:**
	- **Top-down:** Start with the largest items and break them down.
	- **Bottom-up:** Start with specific tasks and roll them up.
	- **Analogy:** Review WBSs of similar projects.
- **Scope Baseline:**
	Comprised of the approved project scope statement, the WBS, and the WBS dictionary.
- **Validation vs. Control:**
	Validation focuses on **acceptance** of deliverables, while Control focuses on **managing changes** to the scope.
- **Critical Metric:**
	Fixing a requirement defect in the operation phase can cost significantly more than fixing it during the initial requirements phase.

---

**Analogy:** Project Scope Management is like **building a house with a strict contract**. The **Scope Statement** is the blueprint that says exactly how many rooms you will have. The **WBS** is the checklist for the builders (foundation, framing, plumbing). **Validating Scope** is the final walkthrough with the owner to get the keys. If the owner suddenly asks for a swimming pool halfway through without a formal agreement, that is **scope creep**, and if the builder adds fancy gold faucets for free just to be nice, that is **gold plating**—both can ruin the project's budget and schedule.
