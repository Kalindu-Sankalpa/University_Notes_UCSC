This topic emphasizes that **IT projects cannot be run in isolation**; they must operate within a broad organizational environment using **systems thinking**, which takes a holistic view of the project's context. To manage effectively, managers use the **Three Sphere Model**, which balances **business, organizational, and technological issues**. Success often depends on navigating the **organizational structure**—whether it is Functional, Project-based, or a Matrix—and understanding the **organizational culture**, which consists of shared values and behaviors.

A critical factor for success is **top management commitment**, as senior executives provide necessary resources, approve unique project needs, and act as "champions" to ensure the project continues to support business needs. Additionally, managers must navigate **recent trends** like globalization, outsourcing, and the use of **virtual teams**.

The technical management of a project is organized into **five process groups**: **initiating, planning, executing, monitoring and controlling, and closing**. These groups interact with **ten knowledge areas**, such as scope, cost, and risk management. Furthermore, organizations must choose an appropriate **development life cycle**, ranging from **Predictive (Waterfall)**, where scope is fixed early, to **Adaptive (Agile)**, which focuses on iterative delivery and close collaboration.

# Summary

- **Systems Thinking:** A holistic approach involving systems philosophy (model for thinking), systems analysis (problem-solving), and systems management (addressing business/org/tech issues).

- **The Three Spheres:** **Business** (costs/impact), **Organization** (who is affected/trained), and **Technology** (hardware/software specifications).

- **Organizational Structures:**
	1. **Functional:** Managers report to the CEO.
	2. **Project:** Program managers report to the CEO; high PM authority.
	3. **Matrix:** Middle ground where personnel often report to two or more bosses.

- **Process Groups (The "How"):**

    1. **Initiating:** Defining and authorizing the project.
    2. **Planning:** Devising a workable scheme to meet organizational needs.
    3. **Executing:** Coordinating people and resources.
    4. **Monitoring & Controlling:** Measuring progress and taking corrective action.
    5. **Closing:** Formalizing acceptance and ending the project.

- **Product Life Cycles:**
    1. **Predictive (Waterfall):** Scope, schedule, and cost are determined early.
    2. **Iterative/Incremental:** Scope is determined early but estimates/functionality are refined through repeated cycles.
    3. **Adaptive (Agile):** Stakeholders define scope before each iteration; focus on frequent delivery of useful results.

- **Important Terms:**
	- **Phase Exits/Kill Points:** Management reviews after each phase to evaluate progress and decide if a project should continue.
	- **Champion:** A senior manager who acts as a key proponent for a project.

---

> [!NOTE] Analogy
> Running an IT project within an organization is like **navigating a ship through a busy harbor**. You cannot just focus on your own engine and crew (**the process groups**); you must also understand the tides and currents of the surrounding waters (**organizational culture**), obey the harbor's traffic laws (**organizational structure**), and ensure you have the lighthouse's guidance and the harbor master's permission (**top management commitment**) to reach your destination safely.
