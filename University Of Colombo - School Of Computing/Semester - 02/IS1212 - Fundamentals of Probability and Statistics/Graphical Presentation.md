**Graphical representation** is the art of turning messy raw data into useful information that is easy to read, understand, and use for comparisons. Since raw data alone provides very little insight, we use **charts, plots, and tables** to grab an audience's attention and illustrate important facts clearly.

# 1. Presenting Categorical Data

When dealing with categories (like gender or district), we organize data into **Summary Tables** for one variable or **Contingency Tables** for two variables.

- **Bar/Column Charts:** Each category is represented by a bar where the **height represents the count or percentage**.
	_Fancy Example:_ Imagine a bar chart showing the favorite pizza toppings of 100 people; the "Pepperoni" bar stands tallest, showing it's the crowd favorite.

- **Pie Charts:** A circle divided into "slices" representing parts of a whole.
	_Fancy Example:_ Think of your monthly budget as a giant pie; the "Rent" slice might take up half the circle, while "Entertainment" is just a tiny sliver.

- **Pareto Chart:** Bars are arranged in **decreasing order** of frequency. It follows the **80/20 rule**, suggesting that roughly 80% of effects (e.g., software bugs) come from 20% of causes (e.g., specific code modules).

# 2. Presenting Numerical Data

Numerical data (like height or exam marks) requires different tools to show patterns of variability.

- **Histograms:** A set of adjacent rectangles where the **area is proportional to the frequency**. It is the best tool to understand the "shape" of your data.

- **Stem-and-Leaf Plots:** A quick way to see a distribution by splitting numbers into "stems" (leading digits) and "leaves" (trailing digits).
	_Fancy Example:_ If you have test scores like 82, 85, and 91, the "8" is the stem with "2" and "5" as leaves, while "9" is a new stem with "1" as its leaf.

- **Time Plots & Line Charts:** Used when data has a meaningful sequence, like time, to find **trends** (rise/fall) or **seasonal variations** (repeating patterns).

# 3. Relationships and "Misfits"

- **Scatter Diagrams:** These plots use ordered pairs (X,Y) to show the **relationship between two continuous variables**.
	_Positive Relationship:_ As X goes up, Y goes up (e.g., study hours vs. exam marks).
	_Negative Relationship:_ As X goes up, Y goes down (e.g., speed vs. travel time).

- **Outliers:** These are the **"misfits"**—observations that lie outside the overall pattern of the data. They are often visible as a large gap in a distribution.

# 4. Principles of Excellent Graphs

A good graph should **never distort data** or contain **"chart junk"** (unnecessary decorations). Key rules include:

- Always **label your axes** and provide a **title**.

- The vertical axis scale should generally **begin at zero** to avoid misleading the viewer.

# Short Note for Quick Recall

|Data Type|Best Chart to Use|Memory Trigger|
|---|---|---|
|**Categorical**|Bar, Pie, Pareto|**BPP:** "Best Pizza Party" (Bar, Pie, Pareto)|
|**Numerical**|Histogram, Stem-and-Leaf|**H-S:** "High Stakes" (Histogram, Stem)|
|**Trends over Time**|Line Chart / Time Plot|**Line = Timeline**|
|**Relationships**|Scatter Diagram|**Scatter = Dots everywhere**|
|**Priority/Impact**|Pareto Chart|**80/20 Rule**|

# Analogy for Understanding:

Organizing data is like **cleaning a messy room.** Raw data is the pile of clothes on the floor. A **Summary Table** is like folding them into neat stacks. A **Bar Chart** is like hanging them up by color so you can instantly see which color you own the most of. If you see one neon-green sock in a pile of black ones, that’s your **Outlier**!
