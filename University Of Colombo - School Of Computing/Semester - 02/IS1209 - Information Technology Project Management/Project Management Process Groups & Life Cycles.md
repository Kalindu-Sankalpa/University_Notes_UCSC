>This topic primarily covers **Project Management Process Groups and the Project Life Cycle** within the context of Information Technology

To understand this topic, I must distinguish between the "what" (the framework) and the "how" (the execution).

# 1. The Five Process Groups
Think of these as the stages every project must go through, regardless of its size.

- **Initiating:**
	Defining and authorizing the project or a new phase.

- **Planning:**
	Devising a workable scheme to ensure the project meets organizational needs. **"Alpha" project managers** (the top 10%) are noted for spending significantly more time here **21% of their time versus 11%** for average managers—which leads to smoother execution.

- **Executing:**
	Coordinating people and resources to carry out the plan.

- **Monitoring and Controlling:**
	Regularly measuring progress and taking **corrective action** to keep the project on track.

- **Closing:**
	Formalizing acceptance of the product and ending the project or phase efficiently.

## 2. Choosing a Life Cycle
A **Project Life Cycle** is a collection of phases (starting, organizing, carrying out, and finishing). Selecting the right model depends on two factors: the **degree of requirement change** and the **frequency**.

### Short Note for Study of delivery.

- **Predictive (Waterfall):** Best when requirements are stable. Scope, schedule, and cost are determined early.

- **Iterative:** Scope is set early, but time/cost estimates are modified as the team's understanding of the product grows.

- **Incremental:** Functionality is added through a series of iterations.

- **Adaptive (Agile/Change-Driven):**
	Best for high-change environments. Stakeholders define the scope before each iteration, producing a usable product frequently.

## 3. Pre-Initiation and Initiation

Before a project officially starts, senior management performs **pre-initiation tasks**, such as identifying the project sponsor, selecting a Project Manager, and developing a **Business Case.**

The business case justifies the investment using financial metrics like **Net Present Value (NPV)** and **Return on Investment (ROI)**. Once authorized, the main outputs of initiation are the **Project Charter** and a **Stakeholder Register**.

## 4. Standards vs. Methodologies

This clarify a common point of confusion:
- **Standard:** A guide (like the **PMBOK**) that describes "what" should be done.

- **Methodology:** A specific set of procedures describing "how" things should be done (e.g., **PRINCE2, Agile, or Six Sigma**).

# Short Note for Study

**Topic: PM Process Groups & Life Cycles**

- **10 Knowledge Areas:** Integration, Scope, Schedule, Cost, Quality, Resource, Communications, Risk, Procurement, and Stakeholder.

- **5 Process Groups:** Initiating, Planning, Executing, Monitoring/Controlling, and Closing.

- **Key Insight:** Effective monitoring requires re-examining the business need in every phase to decide if the project is still worth continuing.

- **Life Cycle Types:**
	- _Predictive:_ Low change, low delivery frequency.
	- _Adaptive (Agile):_ High change, high delivery frequency.

- **Important Documents:**
	- _Business Case:_ Financial justification (NPV/ROI).
	- _Project Charter:_ Formalizes the project's existence.
	- _Stakeholder Register:_ Lists everyone involved or affected.

- **Management Reviews:**
	Also called **phase exits** or **kill points**, these are critical for evaluating progress and determining if a project should be continued, redirected, or terminated.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Analogy:** Managing a project using these groups is like **writing a book**. **Initiating** is getting a contract from a publisher; **Planning** is creating the outline and chapter breakdown; **Executing** is the actual writing; **Monitoring and Controlling** is the editing process where you check for plot holes and fix them; and **Closing** is sending the final manuscript to the printer and receiving your first copy.
