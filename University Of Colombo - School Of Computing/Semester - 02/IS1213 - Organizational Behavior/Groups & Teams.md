>**Groups and Teams**, distinguishing between simple collections of individuals and highly coordinated units that achieve synergy.

# 1. Defining and Classifying Groups

A **group** consists of two or more interacting and interdependent individuals who come together to achieve specific objectives. Groups are classified into two main categories:

- **Formal Groups:**
  Defined by the organization's structure with designated work assignments (e.g., **Command groups** who report to a manager, or **Task groups** working on a specific job).

- **Informal Groups:**
  Natural social alliances that appear in response to a need for contact (e.g., **Interest groups** seeking a specific objective or **Friendship groups** based on common characteristics).

**Social Identity Theory** suggests people join groups because their self-esteem becomes tied to the group's performance, driven by factors like similarity, status, and uncertainty reduction.

# 2. Models of Group Development

Groups typically evolve through the **Five-Stage Model**:

1. **Forming:** Uncertainty as members determine their roles.

2. **Storming:** Intragroup conflict as roles are developed.

3. **Norming:** Development of close relationships and cohesiveness.

4. **Performing:** The group becomes fully functional.

5. **Adjourning:** Wrapping up activities in temporary groups.

Alternatively, temporary groups with deadlines follow the **Punctuated-Equilibrium Model**, characterized by long periods of inertia followed by a burst of activity at the halfway point.

# 3. Key Group Properties

Several properties shape group behavior:

- **Roles:** Expected behavior patterns. Mismatches between **role perception** and **role expectations** can lead to conflict.

- **Norms:** Shared acceptable standards of behavior. The **Hawthorne Studies** demonstrated that group norms influence productivity more than the physical environment.

- **Status:** A socially defined rank that differentiates members. High-status members often feel less pressure to conform to norms.

- **Size:** Smaller groups are faster at completing tasks, but larger groups are better at problem-solving. Managers must guard against **social loafing**, the tendency for individuals to expend less effort when working collectively.

- **Cohesiveness:** The degree to which members are attracted to one another.

# 4. Contrasting Groups and Teams

While the terms are often used interchangeably, they differ significantly in their goals and synergy:

- **Work Groups:**
  Interact primarily to share information; there is **no joint effort** required, resulting in neutral synergy.

- **Work Teams:**
  Generate **positive synergy** through coordinated effort; the collective performance is greater than the sum of individual inputs.

# 5. Types of Teams and Effectiveness

Organizations typically use four types of teams: **Problem-solving**, **Self-managed**, **Cross-functional** (different work areas at the same level), and **Virtual** (physically dispersed members using technology).

According to the **Team-Effectiveness Model**, success depends on three categories:

- **Context:** Adequate resources, effective leadership, a climate of trust, and reward systems reflecting team contributions.

- **Composition:** Member abilities, personality traits (like Conscientiousness), and an optimal size of **5 to 9 members**.

- **Process:** Commitment to a common purpose, specific goals, and minimizing social loafing.

# 6. Managerial Implications

To build effective teams, managers should select individuals with interpersonal skills, provide training for team-based work, and rework reward systems to encourage cooperation over competition. Notably, teams are not always the answer; they should only be used if the work is complex, requires interdependent tasks, and creates a common purpose larger than individual goals.
