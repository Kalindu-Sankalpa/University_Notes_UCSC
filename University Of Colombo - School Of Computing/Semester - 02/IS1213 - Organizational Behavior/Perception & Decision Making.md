# The Nature of Perception

**Perception** is the process by which individuals organize and interpret sensory impressions to give meaning to their environment. A core tenet of Organizational Behavior is that **people's behavior is based on their perception of reality, not on reality itself**. Therefore, the world as it is perceived is the world that is behaviorally important.

Perception is influenced by three main factors:

- **The Perceiver:** Their attitudes, motives, interests, experience, and expectations.
- **The Situation:** Contextual factors like time, work setting, and social setting.
- **The Target:** Characteristics of what is being observed, such as novelty, motion, sounds, size, and background.

# Attribution Theory - Judging Others

When we observe others, we attempt to determine if their behavior is **internally caused** (under their control) or **externally caused** (resulting from outside situations). This is determined by three factors:

1. **Distinctiveness:** Does the person show different behaviors in different situations?.
2. **Consensus:** Does everyone facing a similar situation respond in the same way?.
3. **Consistency:** Does the person respond the same way over time?.

We often fall victim to two major errors:

- **Fundamental Attribution Error:**
	The tendency to underestimate external factors and overestimate internal factors when judging others (blaming the person, not the situation).
- **Self-Serving Bias:**
	Attributing our own successes to internal factors while blaming failures on external factors.

## Shortcuts in Judging Others

Individuals use several shortcuts that can lead to perceptual errors:

- **Selective Perception:** Interpreting what one sees based on one's own interests and background.
- **Halo Effect:** Drawing a general positive impression based on a single characteristic.
- **Contrast Effect:** Evaluating someone by comparing them to others recently encountered.
- **Stereotyping:** Judging someone based on the perception of the group to which they belong.

# Decision-Making Models

Decisions are choices made from alternatives, and every step of the process is influenced by perception. The sources identify three models:

- **Rational Decision Making:**
	A "perfect world" six-step model that assumes complete information and maximum payoff.
	
	> [!important] perfect world - six steps
	>  1. Define the problem.
	>  2. Identify the decision criteria.
	>  3. Allocate weight to the criteria.
	>  4. Develop the alternatives.
	>  5. Evaluate the alternatives.
	>  6. Select the best alternative.

- **Bounded Rationality:**
	The "real world" model where individuals "satisfice"—seeking solutions that are satisfactory and sufficient given limited data.
- **Intuition:**
	A non-conscious process created from distilled experience that results in rapid, affectively charged decisions.

# Decision Biases and Errors

Common biases that distort rational decision-making include:

- **Overconfidence Bias:** Believing too much in one's own ability.
- **Anchoring Bias:** Relying too heavily on the first information received.
- **Confirmation Bias:** Selecting only facts that support our preconceived decisions.
- **Escalation of Commitment:** Staying with a decision even when there is clear evidence it is wrong.
- **Hindsight Bias:** Falsely believing we could have predicted an outcome after it is already known.

# Ethics and Creativity

Decision-making also involves **ethical criteria**:

- **Utilitarianism:** Seeking the greatest good for the greatest number.
- **Rights:** Respecting and protecting the basic liberties of individuals.
- **Justice:** Enforcing rules fairly and impartially.

Finally, **creativity** is the ability to produce novel and useful ideas. The **three-component model of creativity** suggests it results from a mixture of **expertise**, **creative-thinking skills**, and **intrinsic task motivation**
