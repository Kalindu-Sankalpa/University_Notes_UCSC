# Understanding Personality

>[!quote]
>Personality is defined as the **"sum total of ways in which an individual reacts and interacts with others"** and the measurable traits they exhibit.

- **Determinants:** Research suggests that **heredity** (genes) is the primary source of personality, as evidenced by twin studies. While some change can occur over time, traits are generally enduring.
- **Measurement:** The most common method for measuring personality is **self-reporting surveys**, though **observer-ratings surveys** are often better at predicting job success.

# Major Personality Frameworks

The sources highlight two dominant frameworks used to describe personality in organizations:

- **Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI):** This is the world’s most widely used instrument. It classifies individuals on four axes: **Extroverted vs. Introverted, Sensing vs. Intuitive, Thinking vs. Feeling, and Judging vs. Perceiving**. While useful for self-awareness and counseling, the sources note it should not be used as a primary selection tool for hiring.
- **The Big Five Model:** This framework is highly regarded for its ability to predict work behavior. It consists of five dimensions:
	- **Extroversion:** Comfort level with relationships.
	- **Agreeableness:** Propensity to defer to others.
	- **Conscientiousness:** A measure of reliability; highly conscientious people are often the best performers.
	- **Emotional Stability:** Ability to withstand stress.
	- **Openness to Experience:** Range of interests and fascination with novelty.

# Other Relevant Traits

Beyond the Big Five, other traits impact Organizational Behavior:

- **Self-Monitoring:** The ability to adjust behavior to external, situational factors.
- **Proactive Personality:** Individuals who identify opportunities, show initiative, and persevere until change occurs.
- **Type A vs. Type B:** Type A individuals are in an "incessant struggle to achieve more in less time," while Type B individuals are the opposite.
- **Risk Taking:** The willingness to take chances, which can be an asset or a liability depending on the job requirements.

# Values in the Workplace

Values represent basic convictions that a specific mode of conduct or end-state is personally or socially preferable. They are essential because they:

- **Influence perception** of the world around us.
- Represent an individual's interpretation of **"right" and "wrong"**.
- Provide a foundation for understanding **attitudes and motivation**.

# Linking Personality and Values to the Environment

Managers are increasingly focused on how well a person "fits" within the broader context of the organization:

- **Person-Job Fit:** Using **John Holland’s typology**, organizations aim to match six personality types (Realistic, Investigative, Social, Conventional, Enterprising, and Artistic) to congruent occupations to increase satisfaction and reduce turnover.
- **Person-Organization Fit:** This suggests that employees are attracted to and selected by organizations that match their values, and mismatches often lead to turnover.

# Global and Cultural Implications

The sources emphasize that values and personality perceptions vary globally. **Hofstede’s Framework** is used to assess national cultures across six dimensions: 

>**Power Distance, Individualism vs. Collectivism, Masculinity vs. Femininity, Uncertainty Avoidance, Long-term vs. Short-term Orientation, and Indulgence vs. Restraint**. 

Understanding these dimensions is vital for managing diverse, international teams effectively.
