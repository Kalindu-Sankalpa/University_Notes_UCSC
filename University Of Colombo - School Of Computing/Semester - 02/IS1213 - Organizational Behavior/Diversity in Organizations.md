# The Two Levels of Diversity

In the workplace, diversity is generally recognized at two distinct levels:

- **Surface-level diversity:** These are characteristics that are **easily observed**, such as race, gender, and age. These often represent the initial cues we use to categorize people but do not necessarily reflect how they think or feel.
- **Deep-level diversity:** These represent more complex aspects that are **difficult to see at first glance**, such as an individual’s values, personality, and work preferences. As people get to know one another, these deeper traits become more important than surface-level similarities.

After studying this topic, you should be able to:

- **Define key biographical characteristics** and explain their relevance to Organizational Behavior.
- **Define intellectual ability** and contrast it with physical ability.
- **Explain how culture affects** our understanding of these characteristics and abilities.
- **Describe how organizations can manage diversity effectively** to eliminate unfair discrimination.

# Biographical Characteristics

This identify several objective and easily obtained personal characteristics that impact the workplace:

- **Age:** With an aging workforce, older workers bring extensive experience and a strong work ethic, though they sometimes face a perception that productivity declines with age.
- **Gender:** Research indicates **few differences** between men and women that actually affect job performance, although women may more frequently seek flexible work schedules.
- **Race and Ethnicity:** Employees tend to identify more with those similar to themselves, which can sometimes lead to biased opportunities depending on a supervisor's preferences.
- **Tenure:** This refers to seniority or time spent on the job. Employees with longer tenure are often more productive and less likely to leave the organization.
- **Other Factors:** This includes **religion**, which may impact scheduling or dress codes, and **disability**, as more organizations are making active efforts to hire individuals with diverse physical or mental capabilities.

# Ability as Deep-Level Diversity

Ability is defined as an individual’s capacity to perform various job tasks. The sources categorize ability into two factors:

1. **Intellectual Abilities:** These are needed to perform mental activities. Dimensions include **number aptitude, verbal comprehension, perceptual speed, inductive reasoning, deductive reasoning, spatial visualization, and memory**.
2. **Physical Abilities:** These involve the capacity for tasks requiring **stamina, strength, and dexterity**.

# Managing Diversity and Avoiding Discrimination

Diversity management is the practical application of an organization's understanding of diversity. To be successful, organizations must:

- **Eliminate unfair discrimination**, such as sexual harassment, intimidation, mockery, and exclusion.
- Develop **ongoing commitments** at all levels of the organization to capitalize on workforce differences.
- Fine-tune jobs to fit the **specific abilities** of the employees to maximize performance.

By effectively managing diversity, organizations can foster a **more innovative and productive workplace** that is better equipped to serve a diverse global customer base.
