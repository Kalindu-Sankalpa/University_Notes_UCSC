>**Emotions and moods** are essential components of organizational behavior that significantly impact how individuals work, communicate, and interact. Historically, they were ignored due to the **"myth of rationality,"** which viewed emotions as irrational or disruptive to productivity. Today, we recognize that workers cannot separate their feelings from the workplace.

---
# Differentiating Emotions and Moods

The sources use the term **Affect** to cover the broad range of feelings people experience, which is then divided into two categories:

- **Emotions:**
  These are **intense feelings** directed at someone or something. They are caused by **specific events**, are very brief in duration (seconds or minutes), and are often accompanied by distinct facial expressions. There are six basic emotions: **anger, fear, sadness, happiness, disgust, and surprise**.

- **Moods:**
  These are **less intense** feelings that often lack a specific contextual stimulus. They tend to be **more general**, last longer than emotions (hours or days), and are cognitive in nature without distinct facial expressions.

# The Function and Sources of Emotions

Contrary to the old "myth of rationality," emotions are actually **necessary for rational decision-making**, as they allow us to incorporate both thinking and feeling into the process. Our emotions and moods come from several sources:

- **Personality:**
  Some people have a natural tendency to experience certain emotions more intensely.

- **Time and Day:**
  People generally tend to be happier in the midpoint of their daily awake period and toward the end of the week.

- **Stress:**
  Even low levels of constant stress can worsen moods.

- **Social Activities and Physical Health:**
  Socializing, exercise, and adequate sleep generally improve positive moods.

- **Demographics:**
  Age (elderly people tend to have fewer negative emotions) and Gender (women are often more emotionally expressive due to socialization) also play roles.

- **Weather:**
  Interestingly, the sources note that weather is an **"illusory correlation,"** as there is no proven effect of weather on our emotions.

# Key Concepts in the Workplace

- **Emotional Labor:**
  This is when an employee is required to express **organizationally desired emotions** during work transactions. This can lead to **emotional dissonance**, where an employee must project one emotion while feeling another. Managers look at **surface acting** (hiding inner feelings) versus **deep acting** (trying to modify true inner feelings to match expectations).

- **Affective Events Theory (AET):**
  This theory suggests that **work events trigger emotional reactions**, which then influence an employee’s job performance and satisfaction. It highlights that even minor daily hassles or uplifts should not be ignored, as they accumulate over time.

- **Emotional Intelligence (EI):**
  This refers to a person's ability to be **self-aware**, detect emotions in others, and manage emotional cues. While controversial, it is often considered an important factor in job performance.

# Applications in Organizational Behavior

Understanding these concepts allows managers to improve various organizational processes:

- **Selection:** Hiring for high EI, especially for social or customer-facing jobs.

- **Decision Making & Creativity:** Positive moods lead to better, more flexible, and more original thinking.

- **Motivation:** Positive moods increase expectations of success.

- **Leadership:** Leaders who use humor and praise can increase positive moods and "emotional contagion" (catching emotions from others) in the workplace.

- **Negotiation and Customer Service:** Skillful display of emotions can improve service quality and negotiation outcomes.

# Global Implications

While people across cultures generally interpret negative emotions as undesirable and positive ones as desirable, **norms of expression vary**. Some cultures have a bias against emotional expression, while others demand it, making interpretations difficult outside of one’s own culture.
