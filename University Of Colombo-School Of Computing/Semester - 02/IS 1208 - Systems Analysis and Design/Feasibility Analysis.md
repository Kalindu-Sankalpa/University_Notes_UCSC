>Feasibility is a measure of how beneficial and practical the development of an information system will be to an organization. Feasibility analysis is the ongoing process of evaluating this measure at various checkpoints throughout the system's life cycle.
---
# Feasibility Checkpoints

Because the feasibility of a project can change as development progresses, it must be reevaluated at different stages. A project can be canceled, revised, or continued at any of these points:

- **Scope Definition Checkpoint:**
	The analyst measures the urgency of the problem and provides a "first-cut" estimate of development costs to decide if a detailed study is warranted.

- **Problem Analysis Checkpoint:**
	Occurs after a more detailed study, allowing for better estimates of costs and benefits.

- **Decision Analysis Checkpoint:**
	This is the major activity where implementation alternatives are defined (e.g., doing nothing, purchasing software, or building a new system) and analyzed across multiple technical and people-oriented categories.

# The Six Tests of Feasibility

To determine the best solution, analysts use six specific criteria to evaluate candidate systems:

1. **Operational Feasibility:**
	Measures how well a solution meets the identified requirements and if the problem remains worth solving given the solution's cost.

2. **Cultural (or Political) Feasibility:**
	Evaluates how well the solution will be accepted within the organization's climate and how end-users and management feel about their new roles.

3. **Technical Feasibility:**
	Assesses the practicality of the technology, including whether the organization possesses the necessary technical resources and expertise.

4. **Schedule Feasibility:**
	Measures if the project timetable is reasonable and if the system can be implemented within mandatory or desirable deadlines.

5. **Economic Feasibility:**
	A measure of cost-effectiveness, determining if the solution will pay for itself or be profitable.

6. **Legal Feasibility:**
	Ensures the solution aligns with legal and contractual obligations, such as copyright law, labor regulations, and financial standards.

# Cost-Benefit Analysis

This analysis is used to justify a project by weighing its costs against its benefits.

- **Costs:**
	These include one-time development costs (personnel, training, new equipment) and recurring operating costs (computer usage, maintenance).

- **Benefits:**
	These are classified as tangible (easily quantified, such as decreased costs) or intangible (difficult to quantify, such as improved brand image).

# Comparison Tools and the System Proposal

Analysts use two primary matrices to compare candidate systems:

- **Candidate System Matrix:**
	Documents the technical characteristics and differences between various solutions without performing analysis.
	
- **Feasibility Analysis Matrix:**
	Used to rank candidates by assigning weighted scores based on the six feasibility criteria.

The final result of this process is the System Proposal. This is a formal written report or oral presentation intended to "sell" the recommended idea to system owners and users to gain approval for the new system.
